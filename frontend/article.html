Monsoon

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

A monsoon is traditionally a seasonal reversing wind accompanied by corresponding changes in precipitation. The term is now used to describe large-scale seasonal changes in atmospheric circulation and precipitation occurring in certain parts of the world.

History

Strengthening of the Asian monsoon has been linked to the uplift of the Tibetan Plateau after the collision of the Indian subcontinent and Asia around 50 million years ago.

The concept of monsoons has been documented since ancient times, with the term derived from the Arabic word mausim, meaning season. The first scientific studies of monsoon winds were conducted by Arab scholars, like Al-Masudi, who described seasonal wind and ocean current reversals. Sir Edmond Halley later proposed the thermal contrast theory in 1686, explaining the differential heating of land and sea as the cause of monsoons, a concept that has evolved with modern meteorological research.

Ancient and Early References

  • Origin of the Term: The word "monsoon" comes from the Arabic word mausim or mausam, which means "seasonal winds".
  • Early Observers: Early writings by Greek, Indian, Buddhist, and Arab scholars mention monsoons, highlighting their importance for ancient navigation and trade.

Process

Monsoon circulation is driven by differential heating between the land and the ocean, leading to seasonal reversal of wind direction and precipitation patterns.

Global Monsoon

The global monsoon system is the unified, global-scale pattern of seasonal wet and dry periods driven by the annual variation of the Earth's solar radiation and the coupled ocean-atmosphere system. It describes the combined large-scale overturning of the atmosphere in the tropics and subtropics that influences precipitation across various regions worldwide, including Asia, Australia, Africa, and the Americas. This system is characterized by a seasonal shift in wind and rainfall patterns, with distinct regional components (like the Asian, African, and North American monsoons) embedded within the larger global phenomenon.

References

References on monsoons include works like Halley's (1686) historical account of monsoons, P.K. Koteswaram's (1958) research on the Asian Summer Monsoon, and more recent analyses of monsoon dynamics from the India Meteorological Department (IMD) and AGU Publications. Key themes in monsoon research include the role of the Tibetan Plateau, ocean-atmosphere coupling, and the history of human-monsoon interactions, with extensive bibliographies available from institutions like the Indian Institute of Tropical Meteorology (ITM).
